Taj Mahal

The Taj Mahal, a breathtaking testament to love and architectural brilliance, stands as an ivory-white marble mausoleum on the banks of the Yamuna River in Agra, India. Commissioned in 1631 by the Mughal emperor Shah Jahan, it serves as the final resting place of his beloved wife, Mumtaz Mahal. This iconic monument, a masterpiece of Mughal architecture, seamlessly blends Indian, Persian, and Islamic styles.

Construction of the Taj Mahal commenced in 1632 and was completed in 1648, employing an estimated 20,000 workers and artisans. The complex encompasses a vast 17-hectare (42-acre) area, including the main mausoleum, a mosque, a guest house, formal gardens, and a towering gateway.

Great Wall

The Great Wall of China is a chain of fortifications constructed of stone, brick, compressed earth, wooden, and other resources that run east-west across China historical northern borders to protect Chinese states and empires against assaults and invasions by various nomadic peoples from the Eurasian Steppe. Border restrictions, allowing the application of tariffs on products conveyed along the Silk Route, trade restrictions and stimulation, and high migration controls are among the other uses of the Great Wall in China.

Constructed over centuries by countless labourers, this architectural marvel stands as a symbol of the Chinese Empire's determination to protect its borders and preserve its cultural heritage. Stretching for thousands of miles, the Great Wall is not a single, continuous structure, but rather a complex network of walls, watchtowers, and fortresses.

Colosseum

The Colosseum accomplished it. According to organisers, the great Roman monument was named a “New Wonder” of the World along with six other best places on the planet on the night of July 7, 2007, in Lisbon, following a survey supported by Universal Swiss-Canadian filmmaker Bernard Weber and started attending by 100 million citizens from all landmasses. The term Colosseum is thought to have come from a massive statue of Nero that stood nearby for a long time; the statue of Nero was named after the Colossus of Rhodes.

Originally known as the Flavian Amphitheater, the Colosseum was a marvel of engineering and architecture. Its elliptical structure, measuring 620 feet by 513 feet, could accommodate a staggering 50,000 to 80,000 spectators.

Christ The Redeemer

In Portuguese, Christ the Redeemer is also known as the Cristo Redentor. It is a massive statue of Jesus Christ that stands atop Mount Corcovado in Rio de Janeiro, Brazil. Corcovado rises over Rio de Janeiro, Brazil’s Brazil’s main port city, immortalised in traditional and popular music. The Christ the Redeemer monument, which rises 98 feet (30 metres) tall with 92 feet of horizontal spread arms, was finished in 1931. (28 metres). The monument has become a symbol for both Rio de Janeiro and the whole country of Brazil.

Constructed between 1922 and 1931, the statue's imposing figure, with outstretched arms embracing the city below, has captivated the hearts of millions. Its striking design, conceived by French-Polish sculptor Paul Landowski and built by Brazilian engineer Heitor da Silva Costa, is a testament to the collaboration of artistic vision and engineering prowess.

Machu Picchu

Machu Picchu can be built in a “15th century” Inca stronghold perched on a mountain crest 2,430 metres (7,970 feet) above sea level. It is situated above the Scenic Landscape, 80 kilometres (50 miles) northwest of Cuzco and from which the Urubamba River runs in Peru Peru Cusco Region, Urubamba Province Machu Picchu Area. Most Machu Picchu, around 140 buildings, were made of granite, so their ruins blend nicely with the surrounding mountains. Regular granite blocks were fitted so closely collectively (without adhesive) that a knife could not fit between the stones in several spots.

The precise purpose of Machu Picchu remains shrouded in mystery, though theories abound. Some scholars believe it served as a royal estate or sacred religious site for Inca leaders, while others propose it was a retreat for Inca nobility or a centre for astronomical observations.

Chichen Itza

Chichen Itza is a magnificent monument with incredible architectural and historical significance, but it also contains proof that the Mayans were well ahead of their time in Mexico. Researchers have given the building the name Chichen Itza Structure 5B18. While most people are aware that Chichen Itza was created by the Mayans thousands of years ago, there are several features that visitors are unaware of. Chichen Itza’s name refers to an archaeological site rather than the most popular tourist feature, a pyramid.

Among the most iconic landmarks are the El Castillo Pyramid, a massive step pyramid that served as a testament to the Mayans' advanced understanding of astronomy, and the Temple of the Warriors, a grand structure adorned with columns and carvings that depict Mayan deities and warriors, and the Great Ball Court, the largest and most impressive of its kind in the Mayan world, where ritualistic ball games were played.

Petra

Petra, also known as Raqmu by the Nabataeans, is an archaeological and historical city in southern Jordan. The city’s sandstone structure and water pipeline system are well-known. Petra is also known as Rose City because of the stone colour from which it is sculpted.The Nabateans are also noted for their skill in building effective water collection systems in arid deserts and their capability to carve structures into solid rock.

The Nabataeans, a nomadic Arab people, settled in the region around the 4th century BC. They were skilled traders and engineers, and Petra became their capital city, flourishing from the 1st century BC to the 2nd century AD. Its strategic location along the ancient Silk Road made it a vital hub for trade between Arabia, Egypt, and the Mediterranean world.
